Feb-26-20  Petrosianic: <if the world were flat, every airline pilot in the world would know it.>

That's not self-evident either, so let's explain it. If you look at a flat map, and want to find the shortest route from New York to London, what is it? It looks like you should just head straight east from Long Island, and start chugging out over the ocean immediately. But if you ever make that flat and check the route, you'll find that you're still over land hours after takeoff, and somewhere over eastern Canada. On a flat map, it's way out of the way, but on a globe it's actually the shortest route.

The flat earthers know this and have put out their own flat maps that make this journey look correct. BUT, since there's no way to accurately portray a globe on a flat map, any time you minimize the distortion in one place, you <increase> it somewhere else. As a result, these flat earth maps show locations in the southern hemisphere to be insanely far apart. On these flat earth maps, the shortest route from, say, Australia to Tierra del Fuego would be to head NORTH, go over the North Pole, and down through North and South America before you got there. Of course in real life, you could get there in at least a quarter of the distance just by heading east or west.

I've always wanted to meet a southern hemisphere flat earther, and see their maps.

I'm going to be more skeptical about origin stories than about things like the world being round just because strictly speaking they aren't science (neither observable or repeatable). If we, for example, were able to observe life spontaneously generating from non-living matter, that would prove it was possible, and also make it much more plausible that that's how life on earth started. Without that, I'm not going to consider "Well, we can't prove it, but lots of scientists have faith that it happened, so that makes it scientific." Not quite.

Now, Asimov wrote a very good article once, called The Relativity of Wrong, in which he argued that science tends to move in steps from more wrong answers to less wrong ones. As he told it, the curvature of the earth is 8 inches per mile. The flat earth theory says that it's 0. So, the theory is wrong, but in a sense <nearly> right. The perfect sphere theory was also wrong but less wrong than the flat theory, and so on. Then the oblate spheroid theory, which was wrong but less wrong than the sphere theory. We didn't really learn the exact shape of the earth until the 1950's, and there was no word for it.
