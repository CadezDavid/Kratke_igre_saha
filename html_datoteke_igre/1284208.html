Mar-19-12
  FSR: <ounos> The games where Black declines the knight "sacrifice" show that the interpolation of 10.a4 b4 before 11.Nd5 is very useful in (a) making Black's b-pawn weak and (b) creating a potential outpost for White's knight on b6 (secured by the further pawn push a5). These points are illustrated by Reti vs Tartakower, 1919, which continued 11.Nd5 <Qa5> 12.Nxc6 Bxc6 13.Qd4 Nf6 14.Bd2 e5 15.Bxb4 (winning the weak b-pawn) and Nunn vs R Johannes, 1970, which continued 11.Nd5 <Qb8> 12.Nb6 Ra7 13.Nxc6 Bxc6 14.a5 (securing the outpost on b6) Be7 15.Bd2 Nf6 16.Bxb4 (winning the weak b-pawn).

Another possibility for Black was seen in Kupreichik vs B Lindberg, 1992: 10.a4 <Nxd4> 11.Qxd4 bxa4 12.Rxa4. That leaves Black's a-pawn weak, and White won it after 12...Ne7 13.Be3 Nc6 14.Qb6 Qxb6 15.Bxb6 Be7 16.Rea1 Kd7 17.Bf1 Rhb8 18.Bxa6.
